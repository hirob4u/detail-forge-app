# V1.1 Product Decision -- Baseline-First Estimate Architecture

**Status:** Documented, deferred to v1.1
**Date:** March 2026
**Decision owner:** Ben Foran / Foranware

---

## Background

During real-world testing of the v1.0 AI assessment flow against actual vehicle photos, a fundamental limitation of the current architecture became apparent. The AI is asked to generate a complete estimate from scratch based entirely on customer-submitted photos. This creates several problems:

- Customer photos are taken under uncontrolled lighting and angles
- Many conditions cannot be assessed from photos alone (paint thickness, scratch depth, leather type, odor, undercarriage condition)
- The model has a tendency to infer conditions based on vehicle age rather than what photos actually show
- A failed or low-confidence analysis produces nothing useful for the detailer
- The detailer has no structured checklist for what to verify at drop-off

## The Baseline-First Architecture

The v1.1 approach separates the estimate into two phases:

**Phase 1 -- Baseline from vehicle data (immediate, no photos required)**

When the customer submits their vehicle year, make, model, and color, a baseline estimate is generated immediately before photo analysis begins. This baseline is calculated from:

- Vehicle size category (compact, mid-size sedan, full-size sedan, SUV, truck, van)
- Vehicle age bracket
- Standard service pricing for that vehicle category
- Typical condition expectations for that age and type

The baseline gives the detailer a useful starting point even if the photo analysis fails entirely. It also sets a price anchor that the AI assessment adjusts rather than invents.

**Phase 2 -- Photo analysis confirms, adjusts, or flags exceptions**

Instead of generating an estimate from scratch, the AI is given the baseline and asked a more focused question: does this vehicle's condition match the baseline, or are there specific factors that should adjust the price up or down?

This is a more tractable problem. The model is confirming or adjusting rather than inventing. The output is:

- Confirmation that baseline is appropriate, OR
- Specific adjustments with reasoning (e.g. "paint condition is better than expected for age -- reduce correction scope")
- Flags for conditions that require in-person verification before the baseline can be confirmed

**Phase 3 -- Structured verification checklist**

The assessment produces a `requiresInPersonVerification` list (already added to v1.0 output schema as groundwork). At drop-off, the detailer works through this checklist and makes final adjustments to the quote. The customer already has a baseline range so there are no surprises.

## Schema Changes Required for V1.1

New table: `serviceBaselines`
```
id, vehicleCategory, serviceType, basePrice, description, createdAt
```

New column on jobs: `baselineEstimate jsonb`

New column on jobs: `baselineGeneratedAt timestamp`

The analyze route will accept the baseline as input and use it to frame the assessment rather than generating prices independently.

## Why This Is Better

1. The estimate is never zero -- vehicle data alone produces a useful starting point
2. The AI's job is easier and more accurate -- confirming vs inventing
3. Customer expectations are set correctly with a range before photos are even analyzed
4. The detailer has a structured drop-off checklist
5. Failed photo analysis does not kill the entire estimate
6. Pricing becomes more consistent across jobs of similar vehicles

## Deferral Rationale

The v1.0 flow is good enough to demonstrate the concept and close the intake-to-quote loop. The baseline architecture requires additional schema work, a service baseline data model, and a more significant prompt restructure. These changes should be made deliberately as a complete v1.1 feature rather than bolted onto the v1.0 codebase incrementally.

The `requiresInPersonVerification` field added to the v1.0 output schema is deliberate groundwork for this architecture.
